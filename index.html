Mediation Analysis

An interactive tutorial and analysis tool for understanding and computing mediation effects

What is Mediation Analysis?

Mediation analysis examines how an independent variable (X) transmits its effect to a dependent variable (Y) through an intervening variable called a mediator (M). Rather than simply asking "Does X affect Y?", mediation asks "Does X affect Y because X first affects M, which in turn affects Y?"

The indirect effect is the product of path coefficients a × b. The direct effect is c' (the remaining effect of X on Y after accounting for the mediator). The total effect is c = ab + c'.

Indirect effect = a × b
Total effect: c = a × b + c'
The sign of ab matters — it indicates direction, not just magnitude

The Old Way vs. The Modern Way

Baron & Kenny (1986) — Outdated

  1. Show X significantly predicts Y (path c)
  2. Show X significantly predicts M (path a)
  3. Show M predicts Y controlling for X (path b)
  4. Show that X→Y drops (or becomes non-significant) when M is included

Problems: Requires a significant total effect (step 1), which can miss real mediation. Relies on significance testing rather than effect sizes. Distinguishes "full" vs. "partial" mediation — a distinction that is largely meaningless and depends on sample size. Low statistical power for detecting indirect effects.

Modern Approach — Bootstrap (Preacher & Hayes, 2008)

  1. Estimate paths a and b via regression
  2. Compute the indirect effect ab
  3. Bootstrap the sampling distribution of ab (e.g., 5,000 resamples)
  4. Construct a confidence interval for ab. If it does not include zero, the indirect effect is significant

Advantages: Does not require a significant total effect. Does not assume normality of the ab product (which is rarely normal). Higher power. Reports effect sizes and confidence intervals rather than just p-values. No "full" vs. "partial" distinction — just report the indirect effect and its CI.

Why "Full Mediation" is Problematic

The concept of "full mediation" (c' = 0) is statistically problematic. A non-significant direct effect does not mean it is zero — it may simply reflect insufficient power. Whether c' is significant depends on sample size, not on the underlying process. Modern practice recommends reporting the indirect effect (ab), the direct effect (c'), and their confidence intervals, without categorizing mediation as "full" or "partial" (Hayes, 2009; Rucker et al., 2011).

Cross-Sectional vs. Longitudinal Data

This is perhaps the most critical methodological point. Mediation analysis implies a causal chain: X causes M, which causes Y. But causation requires temporal precedence.

Cross-sectional data (all measured at the same time)

When X, M, and Y are all measured at the same time point, you have correlations, not causal effects. The model X → M → Y is statistically indistinguishable from M → X → Y. You cannot determine directionality. While you can still estimate the statistical indirect effect, you cannot make causal claims. Be transparent about this limitation.

Longitudinal data (measured across time points)

To support causal inference, you need temporal separation: X measured at time 1 (t0), M measured at a later time (t1), and Y measured at an even later time (t2). This establishes temporal precedence and strengthens causal arguments (though does not guarantee causality on its own — you also need theory and ideally experimental manipulation).

Recommendations

If you have cross-sectional data: You can still compute the indirect effect and bootstrap CIs, but frame results as "statistical mediation" or "indirect effects," not "causal mediation." State explicitly that the data do not allow for causal inference.

If you have longitudinal data: Measure X at t0, M at t1, and Y at t2. Consider controlling for baseline M (t0) and baseline Y (t0) to account for stability effects (Cole & Maxwell, 2003; Maxwell & Cole, 2007).

Why the Sign of ab Matters

The indirect effect is a × b, not |a × b|. The sign carries important information. When the indirect effect (ab) and the direct effect (c') have opposite signs, this is called inconsistent mediation or a suppression effect. In this case, the total effect (c) can be close to zero even though both the direct and indirect effects are substantial.

Example: Suppression

Suppose coffee intake (X) increases alertness (M) (a = +0.5), and alertness (M) improves performance (Y) (b = +0.4), so ab = +0.20. But coffee also increases anxiety, which hurts performance: c' = −0.18. The total effect c = 0.20 + (−0.18) = 0.02 — nearly zero. Under Baron & Kenny, you would never test for mediation because step 1 fails. Yet a meaningful indirect pathway exists.

How Bootstrapping Works

The product ab does not follow a normal distribution, so traditional methods (e.g., the Sobel test) that assume normality perform poorly. Bootstrapping is a non-parametric approach that approximates the sampling distribution of ab by resampling from the observed data.

  1. Original sample: You have N observations of (X, M, Y). Estimate a, b, and compute ab.
  2. Resample: Draw N observations with replacement from the original data.
  3. Re-estimate: Fit the regression models on the resampled data and compute ab* for this bootstrap sample.
  4. Repeat: Do steps 2–3 many times (typically 5,000 or 10,000) to build a distribution of ab* values.
  5. Confidence interval: Use the percentile method (or bias-corrected/accelerated) to construct a CI. If the 95% CI does not contain zero, the indirect effect is statistically significant.

Multiple Mediators

When multiple mediators are hypothesized, you can estimate specific indirect effects through each mediator, controlling for the others. This is critical because mediators may be correlated, and failing to include all relevant mediators can bias the estimates.

Specific indirect effect through M1 = a1 × b1
Specific indirect effect through M2 = a2 × b2
Total indirect effect = a1b1 + a2b2
Each specific indirect effect is estimated controlling for the other mediator(s)

In the multiple mediator model, the paths b1 and b2 are estimated simultaneously, meaning each mediator's effect on Y is computed while controlling for the other mediator(s). This provides a cleaner estimate of each pathway. You can also contrast specific indirect effects (e.g., is a1b1 larger than a2b2?) by bootstrapping the difference.

Covariates (Confounders)

Covariates are variables that you include in the model to control for potential confounding. If a variable influences both the mediator and the outcome (or the predictor and the mediator), omitting it can bias the path estimates and, consequently, the indirect effect.

When to include covariates

Include variables that are theoretically or empirically related to both the mediator and the outcome. Common examples include demographic variables (age, sex), baseline measures, or other known confounders. In the regression equations, covariates are added to both the M-model and the Y-model. The bootstrap procedure then naturally accounts for them.

M = a0 + aX + f1C1 + f2C2 + eM
Y = b0 + c'X + bM + g1C1 + g2C2 + eY
C1 and C2 are covariates included in both equations

Summary of Best Practices

Do

Use bootstrap confidence intervals for indirect effects. Report ab, c', and their CIs. Use longitudinal designs when possible. Include relevant covariates. Use 5,000+ bootstrap resamples. Report effect sizes.

Don't

Use Baron & Kenny's causal steps. Say "full" or "partial" mediation. Claim causality from cross-sectional data. Use the Sobel test as a primary method. Use |ab| instead of ab. Ignore potential confounders.
